1. Position Statement

In 2017, when I ran as a BC Green Party candidate in Vancouver Fraserview, people often asked why I did not spend more time talking about the environment. My answer was simple:

I do not want to spend my life fighting for the environment. I want to help build a world where we no longer have to.

The challenge before Canada is not choosing between prosperity and sustainability. It is designing economic, technological, and governance systems that make both possible. That perspective informs my views on LNG, renewable energy, nuclear power, critical minerals, Indigenous self-determination, and Canada's role in the global energy transition.

As a retired computer scientist, software engineer, and aspiring polymath, I approach public policy through systems thinking. The question is not merely what policy wins today's argument, but what institutions, incentives, and technologies create a more resilient society for future generations.

3. Guiding Principle: Resilience Through Diversity

Complex systems become fragile when they depend excessively on one technology, one fuel, one ideology, one supplier, one legal mechanism, or one governance model. Resilient societies preserve options, learn from experience, and adapt without abandoning their destination.

That is why the sailing metaphor matters. A competent captain changes tack as winds, currents, reefs, technology, economics, and weather change. But changing tack is not the same as changing destination.

Energy diversity

Hydro, wind, solar, nuclear, storage, geothermal, efficiency, and transitional fuels each have different strengths and risks.

Institutional diversity

Crown corporations, private firms, Indigenous partnerships, federal programs, and regulated utilities each have a role.

Technology diversity

HVDC, storage, critical-mineral processing, SMRs, carbon management, and grid intelligence should all be part of the option set.

Governance diversity

DRIPA, elected governments, Indigenous governments, courts, regulators, and citizens must be coordinated rather than weaponized.

4. Canada as an Energy Sovereignty Partner

Canada does not seek merely to sell energy to the world. Canada seeks to partner with the world. Our objective is not only to strengthen Canadian Energy Sovereignty, but to help our allies and trading partners strengthen theirs as well.

Historically, energy relationships have often been characterized by dependency, vulnerability, and geopolitical leverage. Nations dependent on imported energy can become exposed to coercion, conflict, supply disruptions, and price instability. The emerging energy transition presents an opportunity to build a different model.

Through clean electricity, advanced transmission technologies, responsible resource development, nuclear power, critical minerals, technological innovation, and trusted institutions, Canada can help build a world where more nations enjoy greater energy security and greater energy independence.

In this vision, Canada is not merely an energy exporter. Canada is an Energy Sovereignty Partner.

Success should not be measured only by the volume of energy Canada sells, but by the resilience, prosperity, and sustainability that Canada helps create among its partners. A stronger Canada and a stronger world are not competing objectives. They are mutually reinforcing ones.

5. BC Energy and the BC Energy Act

British Columbia should consider creating a new Crown corporation, BC Energy, governed by a new BC Energy Act. This would not necessarily replace BC Hydro. BC Hydro should remain as an operating utility and public power institution, while BC Energy would provide the broader strategic mandate: energy sovereignty, resilience, long-term planning, interconnection, technology evaluation, Indigenous partnerships, and coordination with federal programs.

There was a time when BC Hydro distributed natural gas; institutions evolve as energy systems evolve. The name "BC Hydro" still describes an important institution, but it does not describe the full scope of the coming energy system. The future includes hydro, wind, solar, nuclear, storage, geothermal, demand management, hydrogen, critical minerals, HVDC corridors, and industrial electrification.

BC Energy should not become a patronage agency. Legislation should require substantial leadership from engineers, scientists, economists, Indigenous governance expertise, and public administration. Elected governments should retain democratic oversight and strategic accountability; operational leadership should be evidence-based and technically competent.

6. Federal-Provincial Coordination

A meeting with an MP's office should connect provincial energy architecture to federal responsibilities. The federal government cannot create BC Energy, but it can support provincial energy sovereignty through infrastructure funding, energy corridors, clean-electricity programs, Indigenous infrastructure financing, nuclear regulation, export strategy, critical-minerals policy, and international trade relationships.

Canada needs provincial energy sovereignty and federal energy coordination. Provincial systems provide assets and jurisdiction; federal policy provides scale, diplomacy, national infrastructure, and international partnership.

7. The Immediate LNG Policy Context

The immediate meeting context is public concern over LNG Canada Phase 2 and Ksi Lisims LNG. The activist concern is understandable: large LNG export facilities may lock in decades of methane-gas production and infrastructure. They can affect local ecosystems, Indigenous communities, public finances, electricity demand, and Canada's climate commitments.

At the same time, the projects sit inside a larger geopolitical and economic context. Many countries still need gas, especially as they retire coal, replace Russian energy, or stabilize electrical systems while building renewables and storage. Canada's policy question is therefore not merely whether LNG is good or bad. The harder question is whether Canadian LNG can be governed as a genuinely transitional product without undermining long-term decarbonization.

Plain-language summary: The real policy issue is not "LNG forever" versus "no LNG ever." It is whether Canada can design a credible transition pathway that prevents fossil infrastructure from becoming a permanent excuse for delay.

8. LNG as Transitional Fuel: Strengths and Limits

The strongest case for Canadian LNG is relative, not absolute. Natural gas remains a fossil fuel. It produces carbon dioxide when burned, methane leakage is serious, and infrastructure built today can outlive the political promises attached to it. Climate advocates are right to be skeptical of claims that any large new fossil project is automatically compatible with climate goals.

However, the strongest pragmatic argument is that much of the world will continue consuming gas for decades. If that is true, then the relevant comparison is not between LNG and a perfect zero-carbon world. The relevant comparison may be between Canadian LNG and alternative sources that are more carbon-intensive, less regulated, less transparent, less reliable, or more geopolitically coercive.

Canada's potential energy marketing strategy has three parts: cleaner, not clean; dependable; and trustworthy. The risk is greenwashing. A transitional fuel is only transitional if the transition is real.

9. Nuclear Power and BC Energy Sovereignty

British Columbia's longstanding prohibition on nuclear power was, in my view, wrong. BC should not artificially exclude one of the most important low-carbon energy technologies available. The proper policy is not "nuclear at any cost," but evidence-based evaluation of whether modern nuclear power can be safe, cost-effective, publicly accountable, and useful for energy sovereignty.

BC Nuclear Power means BC Energy Sovereignty.

BC faces rising electricity demand from population growth, electrification, transportation, industry, data centres, AI infrastructure, heating, and potentially electrified LNG. Hydro, conservation, wind, solar, storage, geothermal, and transmission all matter. But excluding nuclear entirely narrows BC's options in a world where resilience depends on diversity.

Modern nuclear power should be evaluated through rigorous safety regulation, transparent economics, siting analysis, seismic and water constraints, waste management, Indigenous consent, public accountability, and comparison with alternatives. The point is to preserve option value and make evidence-based decisions.

10. HVDC and the Electron Economy

Advances in high-voltage direct current infrastructure give BC and Canada more options to sell electrons, not just molecules, across long distances. HVDC can support long-distance transmission, interprovincial power corridors, offshore connections, asynchronous grid links, and eventually wider international clean-electricity trade.

Canada should invest in R&D and deployment capability to capitalize on this. The path to environmental protection becomes clearer when Canada can produce abundant clean electricity, move it efficiently, and use it to displace fossil fuels in transport, buildings, industry, heating, mining, data infrastructure, and export-oriented manufacturing.

The future energy economy may involve exporting clean electrons directly, exporting hydrogen or synthetic fuels made from clean electricity, or exporting energy-intensive goods manufactured with low-carbon power. In that world, BC's hydro legacy, nuclear option value, wind potential, storage, and transmission corridors become strategic assets.

11. Critical Minerals and Technological Adaptation

One common objection to renewable energy is that it replaces fossil-fuel dependence with critical-mineral dependence. That objection contains truth, but it is often overstated. China does not have a simple monopoly on critical minerals. Its advantage is more industrial than geological: processing, refining, manufacturing scale, and supply-chain integration. Canada and many allies possess critical mineral resources and can build stronger processing capability than they have today.

Science and engineering continue to reduce dependence on particular materials. When a material becomes expensive, politically risky, or supply-constrained, engineers search for alternatives. Examples include reduced-cobalt battery chemistries, sodium-ion batteries, rare-earth-free motor designs, better recycling, material thrift, and substitutions in electrical systems.

Systems lesson: The long-term competition may not be over who owns the most critical minerals. It may be over who develops the technologies that make some of those minerals less critical.

12. DRIPA and Indigenous Self-Determination

BC's Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act was a step in the right direction. It should be refined, not eliminated. Reconciliation requires more than symbolic recognition; it requires workable governance, clarity, accountability, consent processes, and practical decision-making.

Indigenous rights and title must not be treated as slogans for either side. Some Indigenous leaders and communities oppose LNG and pipeline infrastructure. Others support, benefit from, or co-own resource projects. Ksi Lisims LNG is especially complicated because it is associated with the Nisga'a Nation and Indigenous economic self-determination. Opposition cannot honestly be framed as simply defending Indigenous sovereignty unless it also explains how it respects Indigenous governments that choose development.

The harder question is what governments should do when Indigenous voices are divided, when some nations support a project and others oppose it, or when elected Indigenous governments and grassroots land defenders disagree. DRIPA should evolve into a clearer, more predictable, more legitimate governance framework rather than become another battlefield in partisan politics.

13. Questions for Policymakers

These questions are intended for an MP's office. They are not anti-LNG or pro-LNG. They test whether the policy architecture is credible.

  • What is Canada's destination? Is it merely energy export, or partnership for mutual energy sovereignty?
  • What public funds, guarantees, tax credits, or infrastructure commitments are being requested for LNG Canada Phase 2 or Ksi Lisims?
  • What prevents transitional LNG infrastructure from becoming a 30- or 40-year obstacle to decarbonization?
  • How should Canada respond when some Indigenous governments support a project and other Indigenous communities or land defenders oppose it?
  • Should the federal government encourage provinces to create energy-sovereignty institutions such as BC Energy?
  • What federal R&D and infrastructure investment is needed for HVDC corridors, interties, storage, nuclear, and clean industrial power?
  • How can today's resource revenues build tomorrow's low-carbon industrial capacity?

Closing Thought

Canada's task is not to choose between environmental stewardship and economic prosperity. It is to build institutions, infrastructure, technologies, and partnerships that make both possible.

The objective is not merely Canadian Energy Sovereignty. The objective is Energy Sovereignty Through Partnership.

Canada should strive not only to strengthen its own energy independence and resilience, but also to help its allies and trading partners strengthen theirs.
